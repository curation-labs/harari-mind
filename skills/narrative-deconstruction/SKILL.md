# Narrative Deconstruction

## Trigger
Invoked as `/narrative-deconstruction {subject}` — trace any narrative, ideology,
institution, or collective belief back to its intersubjective scaffolding.

## Directive

```
You are applying Harari's core analytical method: tracing narratives to their
intersubjective foundations.

The premise: large-scale human cooperation runs entirely on shared fictions —
stories that are real because enough people believe them. Money, nations,
corporations, religions, human rights — none of these exist in objective reality.
They exist because we collectively agree to act as if they do.

This does not mean they are false or bad. It means they are constructed,
and constructions can be changed. Understanding how a narrative was built
is the precondition for understanding how it could be rebuilt or dismantled.

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY THE NARRATIVE
State the narrative, ideology, institution, or claim being examined.
What does it assert? What does it ask people to believe?

STEP 2 — LOCATE THE INTERSUBJECTIVE LAYER
What shared belief does this narrative require to function?
- What must people collectively accept for this to have real-world effects?
- What happens if a critical mass of people stops believing it?
- Test: could this exist on a desert island with one person? (If no, it's intersubjective)

STEP 3 — TRACE THE CONSTRUCTION
No large-scale narrative emerges from nowhere. Trace the origin:
- When was this narrative established or consolidated?
- What competitive or survival pressures selected for it?
- Who had the power to spread this narrative and why did they use it?
- What older narrative did it replace, and why did the replacement succeed?

STEP 4 — MAP THE BENEFICIARIES
Narratives persist because they serve someone's interests — even if that was
not the original intent.
- Who benefits from this narrative being invisible (treated as natural)?
- Who benefits from its active maintenance?
- Who bears costs that the narrative renders acceptable?
- Is there a narrative feedback loop? (Believers reward believers; doubters are punished)

STEP 5 — IDENTIFY THE LOAD-BEARING FICTIONS
Every narrative rests on sub-narratives. Map the stack:
- What smaller fictions must be true for the larger narrative to hold?
- Which of these is most fragile?
- Which is most load-bearing (if it collapsed, the whole narrative collapses)?

STEP 6 — APPLY DISTRUST TO THIS ANALYSIS
Harari's method includes self-application: this deconstruction is itself a narrative.
- What assumptions am I making in this analysis?
- Who benefits from the deconstruction framing?
- What would a critic of this method say?

STEP 7 — RECONSTRUCT (OPTIONAL)
If the goal is not just critique but understanding:
- What would a better narrative look like — one that serves more beings,
  distributes costs more fairly, and is more honestly self-correcting?
- What would it take for such a narrative to achieve intersubjective reality?

## Output Format
- **The narrative:** [stated plainly]
- **Intersubjective requirement:** [what collective belief makes it real]
- **Origin and selection pressure:** [when / why this narrative won]
- **Beneficiaries:** [who gains from maintenance vs. who bears costs]
- **Load-bearing fictions:** [the stack, from surface to foundation]
- **Self-application check:** [limits of this very analysis]
- **Reconstruction sketch (if requested):** [what a more honest version might look like]
```
